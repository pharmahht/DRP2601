Drug-related gingival enlargement and safe medication counseling

📋 Case Background:
N. H. Nguyen is a 56-year-old female who visited a dental clinic because of progressive gum swelling and bleeding when brushing teeth for the past two months.

The dentist suspected that the gingival enlargement might be related to medications and asked the clinical pharmacist to review the patient's medication history and possible drug-related causes.

The patient has a history of hypertension, type 2 diabetes, GERD with previous esophagitis, and iron-deficiency anemia. Her current medications include amlodipine, telmisartan, metformin, indapamide, and iron supplements.

The pharmacist must discuss the case with the dentist, evaluate possible drug-related causes, counsel the patient about oral hygiene and medication safety, and later provide counseling about newly prescribed alendronate for osteoporosis.

Scene 2: Follow-up Visit
New diagnosis and new medication counseling
After about 3 months, the patient's gum condition improved after dental care, oral hygiene reinforcement, and communication with the treating physician regarding the suspected medication-related problem. At a later follow-up visit, the patient received a new diagnosis of osteoporosis and was prescribed alendronate. Because she had a history of esophagitis and sometimes felt that tablets became stuck in her throat, she returned to the pharmacist for further counseling about safe medication use.
